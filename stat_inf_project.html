Part 1: Data

Background

The GSS gathers data on contemporary American society in order to monitor and explain trends and constants in attitudes, behaviors, and attributes. Hundreds of trends have been tracked since 1972. The basic purpose of the GSS is to gather information about contemporary American society in order to explain trends in attitudes and behaviors. The GSS is a gold standard of unbiased social science research. For over 40 years researchers and scholars have relied on the data from the GSS to understand observable attributes of different people and groups in America. This research is then used to address issues of public interest and public policy like national spending, immigration, and confidence in various institutions. The GSS is also one of the most popular teaching tools for a variety of subjects in colleges and universities across the country.

Methodology

From the GS5 website, Participants were selected after random selection of households from across the United States. This ensures that the results of the survey are scientifically valid since all households from across the country had an equal chance of being selected for this survey. Selected participants are interviewed one-one in their homes, or in another place such as a coffee shop or library. If needed, over the phone is also an option for participants. The survey is currently conducted every two years.

Observations on Generalizability, Causality, and Bias.

There is random sampling since every household is likely be surveyed, hence results are generaliazable to the whole population. GS5 is an observational study since there is no random assignment so only association can be inferred but not causation

However, there may be some biases present: * There may be under-reporting and over-reporting since responses are categorical i.e. downplaying when undesirable and exaggerating when desirable. * Selected participants may choose not to respond and decline the survey

Part 2: Research question

Is there a relationship between respondent's religious preference and their political party of affiliation ?

This is an interesting question as this explores how religious preference is associated with political party. It may be interesting to note whether some religious preference are more likely to be affiliated to a particular party.

To analyse this research questions, Variables need are relig, partyid and year

q1 <- select(gss, year, relig, partyid) %>%
  na.omit()%>%
  filter(relig != "None") %>%
mutate(recent = ifelse(year >= "2006", "R", "O") )

Part 4: Inference

We perform statistical inference on the results.

  • Hypothesis

The null hypothesis is that the respondent’s party affiliation and their religious preference are independent variables.

The alternative hypothesis is that the respondent’s party affiliation and their religious preference are dependent.

  • Statistical Method

Since the dataset consists of two categorical variables (religious preference and reported party affiliation), the most appropriate test to be used is the chi-square test of independence. This is used when comparing 2 categorical variables where one of the variables has more than 2 levels

  • Check for conditions

Independence: This is assumed to be true based on the sampling methodology used in the GSS, as it uses random sampling. Furthermore, the size of the sample is less than 10% of the population, and each result is only counted in one cell.

Sample size. As can be seen below, there are at least 5 counts for each cell.

  • Perform the inference tests
chisq.test(q1$relig,q1$partyid)
## Warning in chisq.test(q1$relig, q1$partyid): Chi-squared approximation may be
## incorrect
## 
##  Pearson's Chi-squared test
## 
## data:  q1$relig and q1$partyid
## X-squared = 1220.3, df = 77, p-value < 2.2e-16

This resulted in an approximation error because many of the expected values will be very small and therefore the approximations of p may not be right.

Therefore, let us use simulate p values.

chisq.test(q1$relig,q1$partyid, simulate.p.value = TRUE)
## 
##  Pearson's Chi-squared test with simulated p-value (based on 2000
##  replicates)
## 
## data:  q1$relig and q1$partyid
## X-squared = 1220.3, df = NA, p-value = 0.0004998

Results

The high X-squared statistic with 77 degrees of freedom leads to very low p-value i.e. 0.0004998. Since the p-value is below alpha (0.05), we can conclude that there is sufficient evidence to reject null hypothesis.

In the context of the research question, it means that there is evidence that the party affiliations vary by religious preference.

However, this result cannot be used to determine causality.
